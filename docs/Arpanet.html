History of ARPANET

WHO

  • Developed by: The United States Department of Defense’s ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency), specifically its Information Processing Techniques Office (IPTO).
  • ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency): Funded and oversaw the development of ARPANET. ARPA is part of the U.S. Department of Defense.
  • Key Figures: J.C.R. Licklider, Robert Kahn, Vint Cerf, Leonard Kleinrock, Bob Taylor, and Larry Roberts.

WHAT

  • ARPANET: The first operational packet-switching network, which laid the groundwork for the modern internet.
  • Packet Switching: A method of grouping data into packets that are sent over a network and reassembled at the destination, allowing efficient data transmission.
  • Creation of ARPANET: Designed to allow multiple computers to communicate over long distances without requiring a direct connection.

WHY

  • Need for Communication: To facilitate communication and resource sharing among research institutions and military organizations in the U.S. during the Cold War.
  • Decentralization: To create a communication system that could withstand potential disruptions (like a nuclear attack), making it resilient against failures.
  • Resource Sharing: The goal was to allow researchers to share expensive computing resources.
  • Military Necessity: Ensured communication survivability during military conflict, specifically nuclear attacks.

WHEN

  • Conceptualization: The idea for ARPANET was proposed in the early 1960s, with planning starting in 1966.
  • First Successful Connection: The first message was sent between UCLA and Stanford Research Institute on October 29, 1969.
  • Expansion: Through the 1970s and 1980s, ARPANET connected universities and government research facilities.

WHERE

  • United States: ARPANET initially connected research institutions in the U.S.
  • First Nodes: The first four nodes were located at UCLA, Stanford Research Institute (SRI), UC Santa Barbara, and the University of Utah.

HOW

  • Funding and Development: ARPA funded research at universities and private contractors.
  • Network Design: ARPANET used a decentralized architecture to ensure communication resilience.
  • Packet Switching: Data was broken into packets, sent individually across the network, and reassembled at the destination.
  • First Message: On October 29, 1969, the first message sent was “LO” (the system crashed after sending two letters of "LOGIN").

EXAMPLES AND CONTRIBUTIONS

  • First Message: The first successful message sent was “LO” between UCLA and Stanford.
  • Email: Introduced in 1971 by Ray Tomlinson, allowing users to send messages across ARPANET.
  • TCP/IP Implementation: Vinton Cerf and Robert Kahn developed the TCP/IP protocol suite, adopted in 1983, forming the backbone of the modern internet.
  • Networking Protocols: ARPANET developed protocols like FTP and Telnet for file sharing and remote access.

IMPORTANCE

  • Foundation of the Internet: ARPANET demonstrated the feasibility of packet-switching networks and laid the groundwork for the internet.
  • Decentralized Communication: Ensured communication resilience, a key concern during the Cold War.
  • Collaboration: Fostered interdisciplinary collaboration among scientists, engineers, and researchers.
  • Innovation: ARPANET provided a platform for applications like email, file sharing, and remote access, which continue to influence technology today.
